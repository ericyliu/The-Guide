# On Being Bad at Things

## We like to think we’re okay with being bad at things.

We say we value learning, and we say we want growth, but what we usually mean is that we’re comfortable being bad for a short, bounded period of time. We expect that if we try, we’ll get decent fairly quickly, and that early confusion will resolve itself before it has time to feel uncomfortable.

The problem is that not all domains behave that way.

## When Growth Feels Like Regression

When we move into something harder, the experience doesn’t feel like growth. It feels like loss.

We go from an environment where we can rely on intuition to one where that intuition doesn’t apply. We know how to build things, but we don’t understand the constraints they operate under. We can recognize patterns, but we don’t know which ones matter. The surface looks familiar, but the underlying structure is different enough that our previous experience doesn’t transfer cleanly.

## So we slow down.

- We hesitate where we used to act quickly
- We ask questions we feel like we “shouldn’t” need to ask
- We lose the sense of momentum we’re used to

On paper, this is exactly what we signed up for. In practice, it feels like we got worse.

## The Expectation Mismatch

We tend to model learning based on environments with tight feedback loops.

In games, the rules are clear and the system is designed to be learned. We make a mistake, we see the outcome, and we adjust. Improvement happens quickly enough that we can feel it, and that feeling reinforces the process.

So we internalize a pattern:

1. Effort leads to rapid feedback
2. Feedback leads to visible improvement
3. Improvement happens on short timescales

But real systems are not designed that way.

- They are larger and less visible
- Feedback is delayed or ambiguous
- Progress is incremental and hard to measure

If we carry over the expectation of quick competence, the absence of it feels like failure.

## The Fixed Interpretation

If our baseline assumption is that we should become competent quickly, then not doing so creates a quiet but persistent tension.

We don’t explicitly think “I am failing,” but we notice:

- how long things are taking
- how often we feel confused
- how much we rely on others

And we interpret those signals negatively.

The discomfort doesn’t come from the difficulty itself. It comes from what we think that difficulty says about us.

## The Illusion of “Simple” Work

Work is often framed as extending an existing pattern or applying something that has already been done.

From the outside, this sounds straightforward.

From the inside, small differences compound:

- A system that handles one input behaves differently from one that handles many
- A clean abstraction becomes messy when integrated into a larger pipeline
- Edge cases dominate the actual implementation

What looks like repetition is often reconstruction.

We’re not copying a solution. We’re rediscovering why it works.

## The Uncomfortable Middle State

We end up in a state that is hard to evaluate.

- We are not clearly failing
- We are not clearly succeeding
- We are making progress, but it’s hard to see

We are learning, but not at the pace we expect. We are contributing, but without the confidence we’re used to.

This is the part we tend to resist.

Not because it is inherently bad, but because it lasts longer than we think it should.

## A Different Frame

We don’t actually dislike being bad at things.

We dislike being bad at things for longer than we expect.

If we adjust that expectation, the experience changes.

Instead of: *“I should be good at this by now”*

We shift to: *“This will take longer than I want”*

That small change reduces the gap between expectation and reality. And most of the stress comes from that gap.

## Exercises

These are not about speeding up learning. They are about aligning expectations with reality.

1. Time Horizon Reset
Estimate how long you think something “should” take. Then multiply it by 2–3x.
Use that as your working expectation.

2. Track Invisible Progress
At the end of each day, write down:

- one thing you understand that you didn’t before
- one question that became more precise

This shifts focus from outcomes to understanding.

3. Normalize Confusion
When you feel stuck, explicitly label it:

- “This is what learning here looks like”

This interrupts the instinct to interpret confusion as failure.

4. Reframe Tasks
Instead of asking “Can I complete this?”, ask:

- “What assumptions does this depend on?”
- “What part of this system do I not understand yet?”

This turns execution into exploration.

## Success Indicators

Success in these environments does not look like immediate competence.

It looks like subtle shifts:

- We ask more precise questions
- We can explain parts of the system we couldn’t before
- We feel slightly less lost than we did last week
- We rely less on guesswork and more on understanding

Eventually, things start to feel obvious again.

Not because they became simple, but because we changed.

And by the time that happens, we’ve usually forgotten how long it took to get there.
